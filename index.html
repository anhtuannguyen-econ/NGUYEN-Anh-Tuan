About

I am an economist working at the intersection of behavioral economics, environmental economics, and applied econometrics. My research studies how people make decisions under uncertainty, how information and social context shape preferences, and how these behavioral mechanisms translate into real-world outcomes in sustainability and development. A central theme in my work is the adoption of pro-environmental and productivity-enhancing practices—especially in agriculture— where decisions often involve imperfect information, ambiguous risks, and strong social and institutional constraints.

I completed a PhD in Economics at the University of Paris Nanterre (EconomiX) with a thesis titled “Individual Preferences and Organic Adoption Decisions in Uncertain Contexts”, supervised by Prof. Phu Nguyen-Van. In this project, I investigated how risk and ambiguity attitudes, information completeness, and farmers’ perceptions interact to influence the willingness to transition toward organic farming. My broader interest is to build evidence that helps policy makers and development organizations design interventions that are not only effective on average, but also credible, acceptable, and robust to the diversity of preferences observed in the field.

Methodologically, I combine applied econometrics with experimental approaches. I work with survey data, observational datasets, and lab-in-the-field designs to identify causal mechanisms and evaluate policies. I am particularly interested in “decision environments”: how messaging, information design, and institutional settings alter choices and welfare. This naturally connects my research to policy evaluation, behavioral public policy, and empirical industrial organization questions such as market incentives, adoption frictions, and the diffusion of innovations. Geographically, much of my empirical work focuses on Vietnam, while engaging in comparative and international collaborations in Europe and beyond.

Alongside research, I teach econometrics and related quantitative methods at the graduate and undergraduate levels. Teaching matters to me because it is where ideas become tools: I aim to help students learn how to go from a substantive question to a credible research design, how to diagnose model assumptions, and how to communicate results transparently. I also enjoy working in interdisciplinary teams that include researchers, practitioners, and policy stakeholders—especially when projects involve sustainability, climate adaptation, or the design of programs that encourage pro-social and pro-environmental behavior.
